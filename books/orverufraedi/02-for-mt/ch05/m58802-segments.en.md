<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172099877853 -->
Eukaryotic microbes are an extraordinarily diverse group, including species with a wide range of life cycles, morphological specializations, and nutritional needs. Although more diseases are caused by viruses and bacteria than by microscopic eukaryotes, these eukaryotes are responsible for some diseases of great public health importance. For example, the protozoal disease malaria was responsible for 584,000 deaths worldwide (primarily children in Africa) in 2013, according to the World Health Organization (WHO). The protist parasite [[i:Giardia]] causes a diarrheal illness (giardiasis) that is easily transmitted through contaminated water supplies. In the United States, [[i:Giardia]] is the most common human intestinal parasite ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_giardia]]). Although it may seem surprising, parasitic worms are included within the study of microbiology because identification depends on observation of microscopic adult worms or eggs. Even in developed countries, these worms are important parasites of humans and of domestic animals. There are fewer fungal pathogens, but these are important causes of illness, as well. On the other hand, fungi have been important in producing antimicrobial substances such as penicillin. In this chapter, we will examine characteristics of protists, worms, and fungi while considering their roles in causing disease.

Characteristics of Protists

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172102150516 -->
The word [[i:protist]] is a historical term that is now used informally to refer to a diverse group of microscopic eukaryotic organisms. It is not considered a formal taxonomic term because the organisms it describes do not have a shared evolutionary origin. Historically, the {{term}}protists{{/term}} were informally grouped into the “animal-like” {{term}}protozoans{{/term}}, the “plant-like” {{term}}algae{{/term}}, and the “fungus-like” protists such as {{term}}water molds{{/term}}. These three groups of protists differ greatly in terms of their basic characteristics. For example, algae are photosynthetic organisms that can be unicellular or multicellular. Protozoa, on the other hand, are nonphotosynthetic, motile organisms that are always unicellular. Other informal terms may also be used to describe various groups of protists. For example, microorganisms that drift or float in water, moved by currents, are referred to as {{term}}plankton{{/term}}. Types of plankton include {{term}}zooplankton{{/term}}, which are motile and nonphotosynthetic, and {{term}}phytoplankton{{/term}}, which are photosynthetic.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172099718040 -->
Protozoans inhabit a wide variety of habitats, both aquatic and terrestrial. Many are free-living, while others are parasitic, carrying out a life cycle within a host or hosts and potentially causing illness. There are also beneficial symbionts that provide metabolic services to their hosts. During the feeding and growth part of their life cycle, they are called {{term}}trophozoite{{/term}}[[b:s]]; these feed on small particulate food sources such as bacteria. While some types of protozoa exist exclusively in the trophozoite form, others can develop from trophozoite to an encapsulated cyst stage when environmental conditions are too harsh for the trophozoite. A {{term}}cyst{{/term}} is a cell with a protective wall, and the process by which a trophozoite becomes a cyst is called {{term}}encystment{{/term}}. When conditions become more favorable, these cysts are triggered by environmental cues to become active again through {{term}}excystment{{/term}}.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172101802095 -->
One protozoan genus capable of encystment is {{term}}[[i:Eimeria]]{{/term}}, which includes some human and animal pathogens. [[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_Trophozoit]] illustrates the life cycle of [[i:Eimeria]].

<!-- SEG:m58802:caption:OSC_Microbio_05_01_Trophozoit-caption -->

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172102002036 -->
Protozoans have a variety of reproductive mechanisms. Some protozoans reproduce asexually and others reproduce sexually; still others are capable of both sexual and asexual reproduction. In protozoans, asexual reproduction occurs by binary fission, budding, or schizogony. In {{term}}schizogony{{/term}}, the nucleus of a cell divides multiple times before the cell divides into many smaller cells. The products of schizogony are called merozoites and they are stored in structures known as schizonts. Protozoans may also reproduce sexually, which increases genetic diversity and can lead to complex life cycles. Protozoans can produce haploid gametes that fuse through {{term}}syngamy{{/term}}. However, they can also exchange genetic material by joining to exchange DNA in a process called {{term}}conjugation{{/term}}. This is a different process than the conjugation that occurs in bacteria. The term {{term}}protist conjugation{{/term}} refers to a true form of eukaryotic sexual reproduction between two cells of different mating types. It is found in {{term}}ciliate{{/term}}[[b:s]], a group of protozoans, and is described later in this subsection.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172101669261 -->
All protozoans have a plasma membrane, or {{term}}plasmalemma{{/term}}, and some have bands of protein just inside the membrane that add rigidity, forming a structure called the {{term}}pellicle{{/term}}. Some protists, including protozoans, have distinct layers of cytoplasm under the membrane. In these protists, the outer gel layer (with microfilaments of actin) is called the {{term}}ectoplasm{{/term}}. Inside this layer is a sol (fluid) region of cytoplasm called the {{term}}endoplasm{{/term}}. These structures contribute to complex cell shapes in some protozoans, whereas others (such as amoebas) have more flexible shapes ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_locom]]).

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172101754933 -->
Different groups of protozoans have specialized feeding structures. They may have a specialized structure for taking in food through phagocytosis, called a {{term}}cytostome{{/term}}, and a specialized structure for the exocytosis of wastes called a {{term}}cytoproct{{/term}}. Oral grooves leading to cytostomes are lined with hair-like cilia to sweep in food particles. Protozoans are heterotrophic. Protozoans that are {{term}}holozoic{{/term}} ingest whole food particles through phagocytosis. Forms that are {{term}}saprozoic{{/term}} ingest small, soluble food molecules.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172099482905 -->
Many protists have whip-like {{term}}flagella{{/term}} or hair-like {{term}}cilia{{/term}} made of {{term}}microtubules{{/term}} that can be used for locomotion ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_locom]]). Other protists use cytoplasmic extensions known as {{term}}pseudopodia{{/term}} (“false feet”) to attach the cell to a surface; they then allow cytoplasm to flow into the extension, thus moving themselves forward.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172101797316 -->
Protozoans have a variety of unique organelles and sometimes lack organelles found in other cells. Some have {{term}}contractile vacuole{{/term}}[[b:s]], organelles that can be used to move water out of the cell for osmotic regulation (salt and water balance) ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_locom]]). Mitochondria may be absent in parasites or altered to {{term}}kinetoplastids{{/term}} (modified mitochondria) or {{term}}hydrogenosomes{{/term}} (see [[docref:Unique Characteristics of Prokaryotic Cells|m58793]] for more discussion of these structures).

<!-- SEG:m58802:caption:OSC_Microbio_05_01_locom-caption -->
(a) [[i:Paramecium]] spp. have hair-like appendages called cilia for locomotion. (b) [[i:Amoeba]] spp. use lobe-like pseudopodia to anchor the cell to a solid surface and pull forward. (c) [[i:Euglena]] spp. use a whip-like structure called a flagellum to propel the cell.

Taxonomy of Protists

<!-- SEG:m58802:title:fs-id1172101906894-title -->
Amoebozoa

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172102082555 -->
The supergroup {{term}}Amoebozoa{{/term}} includes protozoans that use amoeboid movement. Actin microfilaments produce {{term}}pseudopodia{{/term}}, into which the remainder of the protoplasm flows, thereby moving the organism. The genus {{term}}[[i:Entamoeba]]{{/term}} includes commensal or parasitic species, including the medically important [[i:E. histolytica]], which is transmitted by cysts in feces and is the primary cause of {{term}}amoebic dysentery{{/term}}. Another member of this group that is pathogenic to humans is {{term}}[[i:Acanthamoeba]]{{/term}}, which can cause {{term}}keratitis{{/term}} (corneal inflammation) and blindness. The notorious “brain eating amoeba,” {{term}}[[i:Naegleria fowleri]]{{/term}}, is a considered a distant relative of the Amoebozoa and is classified in the phylum Percolozoa.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172101803193 -->
The {{term}}Eumycetozoa{{/term}} are an unusual group of organisms called {{term}}slime molds{{/term}}, which have previously been classified as animals, fungi, and plants ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_slime]]). Slime molds can be divided into two types: {{term}}cellular slime molds{{/term}} and {{term}}plasmodial slime molds{{/term}}. The cellular slime molds exist as individual amoeboid cells that periodically aggregate into a mobile slug. The aggregate then forms a fruiting body that produces haploid spores. Plasmodial slime molds exist as large, multinucleate amoeboid cells that form reproductive stalks to produce spores that divide into gametes. One cellular slime mold, {{term}}[[i:Dictyostelium]] [[i:discoideum]]{{/term}}, has been an important study organism for understanding cell differentiation, because it has both single-celled and multicelled life stages, with the cells showing some degree of differentiation in the multicelled form. [[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_CellularLC]] and [[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_PlasmodiLC]] illustrate the life cycles of cellular and plasmodial slime molds, respectively.

<!-- SEG:m58802:title:fs-id1172101801712-title -->
Chromalveolata

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172099902528 -->
The supergroup {{term}}Chromalveolata{{/term}} is united by similar origins of its members’ plastids and includes the apicomplexans, ciliates, {{term}}diatoms{{/term}}, and {{term}}dinoflagellates{{/term}}, among other groups (we will cover the diatoms and dinoflagellates in [[docref:Algae|m58805]]). The {{term}}apicomplexans{{/term}} are intra- or extracellular parasites that have an {{term}}apical complex{{/term}} at one end of the cell. The apical complex is a concentration of organelles, vacuoles, and microtubules that allows the parasite to enter host cells ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_apical]]). Apicomplexans have complex life cycles that include an infective sporozoite that undergoes {{term}}schizogony{{/term}} to make many merozoites (see the example in [[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_Trophozoit]]). Many are capable of infecting a variety of animal cells, from insects to livestock to humans, and their life cycles often depend on transmission between multiple hosts. The genus {{term}}[[i:Plasmodium]]{{/term}} is an example of this group.

<!-- SEG:m58802:caption:OSC_Microbio_05_01_apical-caption -->
(a) Apicomplexans are parasitic protists. They have a characteristic apical complex that enables them to infect host cells. (b) A colorized electron microscope image of a [[i:Plasmodium]] sporozoite. (credit b: modification of work by Ute Frevert)

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172102110397 -->
Other apicomplexans are also medically important. {{term}}[[i:Cryptosporidium parvum]]{{/term}} causes intestinal symptoms and can cause epidemic diarrhea when the cysts contaminate drinking water. {{term}}[[i:Theileria (Babesia) microti]]{{/term}}, transmitted by the tick {{term}}[[i:Ixodes scapularis]]{{/term}}, causes recurring fever that can be fatal and is becoming a common transfusion-transmitted pathogen in the United States ([[i:Theileria]] and [[i:Babesia]] are closely related genera and there is some debate about the best classification). Finally, {{term}}[[i:Toxoplasma gondii]]{{/term}} causes {{term}}toxoplasmosis{{/term}} and can be transmitted from cat feces, unwashed fruit and vegetables, or from undercooked meat. Because toxoplasmosis can be associated with serious birth defects, pregnant people need to be aware of this risk and use caution if they are exposed to the feces of potentially infected cats. A national survey found the frequency of individuals with antibodies for toxoplasmosis (and thus who presumably have a current latent infection) in the United States to be 11%. Rates are much higher in other countries, including some developed countries. {{fn}}J. Flegr et al. “Toxoplasmosis—A Global Threat. Correlation of Latent Toxoplasmosis With Specific Disease Burden in a Set of 88 Countries.” [[i:PloS ONE]] 9 no. 3 (2014):e90203.{{/fn}} There is also evidence and a good deal of theorizing that the parasite may be responsible for altering infected humans’ behavior and personality traits. {{fn}}J. Flegr. “Effects of Toxoplasma on Human Behavior.” [[i:Schizophrenia Bull]] 33, no. 3 (2007):757–760.{{/fn}}

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172099721213 -->
The ciliates (Ciliaphora), also within the Chromalveolata, are a large, very diverse group characterized by the presence of {{term}}cilia{{/term}} on their cell surface. Although the cilia may be used for locomotion, they are often used for feeding, as well, and some forms are nonmotile. {{term}}[[i:Balantidium coli]]{{/term}} ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_balan]]) is the only parasitic ciliate that affects humans by causing intestinal illness, although it rarely causes serious medical issues except in the immunocompromised (those having a weakened immune system). Perhaps the most familiar ciliate is {{term}}[[i:Paramecium]]{{/term}}, a motile organism with a clearly visible {{term}}cytostome{{/term}} and {{term}}cytoproct{{/term}} that is often studied in biology laboratories ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_param]]). Another ciliate, {{term}}[[i:Stentor]]{{/term}}, is sessile and uses its cilia for feeding ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_stentor]]). Generally, these organisms have a {{term}}micronucleus{{/term}} that is diploid, somatic, and used for sexual reproduction by conjugation. They also have a {{term}}macronucleus{{/term}} that is derived from the micronucleus; the macronucleus becomes polyploid (multiple sets of duplicate chromosomes), and has a reduced set of metabolic genes.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172101775199 -->
Ciliates are able to reproduce through {{term}}conjugation{{/term}}, in which two cells attach to each other. In each cell, the diploid micronuclei undergo meiosis, producing eight haploid nuclei each. Then, all but one of the haploid micronuclei and the macronucleus disintegrate; the remaining (haploid) micronucleus undergoes mitosis. The two cells then exchange one micronucleus each, which fuses with the remaining micronucleus present to form a new, genetically different, diploid micronucleus. The diploid micronucleus undergoes two mitotic divisions, so each cell has four micronuclei, and two of the four combine to form a new macronucleus. The chromosomes in the macronucleus then replicate repeatedly, the macronucleus reaches its polyploid state, and the two cells separate. The two cells are now genetically different from each other and from their previous versions.

Öomycetes have similarities to fungi and were once classified with them. They are also called {{term}}water molds{{/term}}. However, they differ from fungi in several important ways. Excavata

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172101847862 -->
The third and final supergroup to be considered in this section is the Excavata, which includes primitive eukaryotes and many parasites with limited metabolic abilities. These organisms have complex cell shapes and structures, often including a depression on the surface of the cell called an excavate. The group Excavata includes the subgroups {{term}}Fornicata{{/term}}, {{term}}Parabasalia{{/term}}, and {{term}}Euglenozoa{{/term}}. The Fornicata lack mitochondria but have flagella. This group includes {{term}}[[i:Giardia lamblia]]{{/term}} (also known as [[i:G. intestinalis]] or [[i:G. duodenalis)]], a widespread pathogen that causes diarrheal illness and can be spread through cysts from feces that contaminate water supplies ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_giardia]]). Parabasalia are frequent animal endosymbionts; they live in the guts of animals like termites and cockroaches. They have basal bodies and modified mitochondria (kinetoplastids). They also have a large, complex cell structure with an undulating membrane and often have many flagella. The trichomonads (a subgroup of the Parabasalia) include pathogens such as {{term}}[[i:Trichomonas vaginalis]]{{/term}}, which causes the human sexually transmitted disease {{term}}trichomoniasis{{/term}}. Trichomoniasis often does not cause symptoms in males, but they are able to transmit the infection. In females, it causes vaginal discomfort and discharge and may cause complications in pregnancy if left untreated.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172099922504 -->
The Euglenozoa are common in the environment and include photosynthetic and nonphotosynthetic species. Members of the genus {{term}}[[i:Euglena]]{{/term}} are typically not pathogenic. Their cells have two flagella, a {{term}}pellicle{{/term}}, a {{term}}stigma{{/term}} (eyespot) to sense light, and chloroplasts for photosynthesis ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_Euglenozoa]]). The pellicle of [[i:Euglena]] is made of a series of protein bands surrounding the cell; it supports the cell membrane and gives the cell shape.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172101791180 -->
The Euglenozoa also include the trypanosomes, which are parasitic pathogens. The genus {{term}}[[i:Trypanosoma]]{{/term}} includes [[i:T. brucei]], which causes {{term}}African trypanosomiasis{{/term}} ({{term}}African sleeping sickness{{/term}} and [[i:T. cruzi]], which causes American trypanosomiasis ({{term}}Chagas disease{{/term}}). These tropical diseases are spread by insect bites. In African sleeping sickness, [[i:T. brucei]] colonizes the blood and the brain after being transmitted via the bite of a tsetse fly ([[i:Glossina]] spp.) ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_tryplife]]). The early symptoms include confusion, difficulty sleeping, and lack of coordination. Left untreated, it is fatal.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172101999615 -->
Chagas’ disease originated and is most common in Latin America. The disease is transmitted by [[i:Triatoma]] spp., insects often called “kissing bugs,” and affects either the heart tissue or tissues of the digestive system. Untreated cases can eventually lead to heart failure or significant digestive or neurological disorders.

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172099488995 -->
The genus {{term}}[[i:Leishmania]]{{/term}} includes trypanosomes that cause disfiguring skin disease and sometimes systemic illness as well.

Neglected Parasites

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172097144570 -->
The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) is responsible for identifying public health priorities in the United States and developing strategies to address areas of concern. As part of this mandate, the CDC has officially identified five parasitic diseases it considers to have been neglected (i.e., not adequately studied). These {{term}}neglected parasitic infections (NPIs){{/term}} include {{term}}toxoplasmosis{{/term}}, {{term}}Chagas disease{{/term}}, {{term}}toxocariasis{{/term}} (a nematode infection transmitted primarily by infected dogs), {{term}}cysticercosis{{/term}} (a disease caused by a tissue infection of the tapeworm {{term}}[[i:Taenia solium]]{{/term}}), and {{term}}trichomoniasis{{/term}} (a sexually transmitted disease caused by the parabasalid {{term}}[[i:Trichomonas vaginalis]]{{/term}}).

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172102135224 -->
The decision to name these specific diseases as NPIs means that the CDC will devote resources toward improving awareness and developing better diagnostic testing and treatment through studies of available data. The CDC may also advise on treatment of these diseases and assist in the distribution of medications that might otherwise be difficult to obtain. {{fn}}Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. “Neglected Parasitic Infections (NPIs) in the United States.” http://www.cdc.gov/parasites/npi/. Last updated July 10, 2014.{{/fn}}

<!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172102120468 -->
Of course, the CDC does not have unlimited resources, so by prioritizing these five diseases, it is effectively deprioritizing others. Given that many Americans have never heard of many of these NPIs, it is fair to ask what criteria the CDC used in prioritizing diseases. According to the CDC, the factors considered were the number of people infected, the severity of the illness, and whether the illness can be treated or prevented. Although several of these NPIs may seem to be more common outside the United States, the CDC argues that many cases in the United States likely go undiagnosed and untreated because so little is known about these diseases. {{fn}}Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. “Fact Sheet: Neglected Parasitic Infections in the United States.” http://www.cdc.gov/parasites/resources/pdf/npi_factsheet.pdf{{/fn}}

Should government agencies like the CDC have the same criteria as private pharmaceutical research labs? <!-- SEG:m58802:para:fs-id1172099883921 -->
The protists are a {{term}}polyphyletic{{/term}} group, meaning they lack a shared evolutionary origin. Since the current taxonomy is based on evolutionary history (as determined by biochemistry, morphology, and genetics), protists are scattered across many different taxonomic groups within the domain Eukarya. Eukarya is currently divided into six {{term}}supergroups{{/term}} that are further divided into subgroups, as illustrated in ([[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_taxon]]). In this section, we will primarily be concerned with the supergroups {{term}}Amoebozoa{{/term}}, {{term}}Excavata{{/term}}, and {{term}}Chromalveolata{{/term}}; these supergroups include many protozoans of clinical significance. The supergroups {{term}}Opisthokonta{{/term}} and {{term}}Rhizaria{{/term}} also include some protozoans, but few of clinical significance. In addition to protozoans, Opisthokonta also includes animals and fungi, some of which we will discuss in [[docref:Parasitic Helminths|m58803]] and [[docref:Fungi|m58804]]. Some examples of the Archaeplastida will be discussed in [[docref:Algae|m58805]]. [[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_groups1]] and [[xref:OSC_Microbio_05_01_groups2]] summarize the characteristics of each supergroup and subgroup and list representatives of each.

Key Concepts and Summary

<!-- SEG:m58802:item:fs-id1172099410086-item-1 -->
[[b:Protists]] are a diverse, [[b:polyphyletic]] group of eukaryotic organisms.

<!-- SEG:m58802:item:fs-id1172099410086-item-2 -->
Protists may be unicellular or multicellular. They vary in how they get their nutrition, morphology, method of locomotion, and mode of reproduction.

<!-- SEG:m58802:item:fs-id1172099410086-item-3 -->
Important structures of protists include [[b:contractile vacuoles]], cilia, flagella, [[b:pellicles]], and pseudopodia; some lack organelles such as mitochondria.

<!-- SEG:m58802:item:fs-id1172099410086-item-4 -->
Taxonomy of protists is changing rapidly as relationships are reassessed using newer techniques.

<!-- SEG:m58802:item:fs-id1172099410086-item-5 -->
The protists include important pathogens and parasites.
