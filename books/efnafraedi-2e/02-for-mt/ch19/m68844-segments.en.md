<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm91090512 -->
The behavior of coordination compounds cannot be adequately explained by the same theories used for main group element chemistry. The observed geometries of coordination complexes are not consistent with hybridized orbitals on the central metal overlapping with ligand orbitals, as would be predicted by valence bond theory. The observed colors indicate that the [[i:d]] orbitals often occur at different energy levels rather than all being degenerate, that is, of equal energy, as are the three [[i:p]] orbitals. To explain the stabilities, structures, colors, and magnetic properties of transition metal complexes, a different bonding model has been developed. Just as valence bond theory explains many aspects of bonding in main group chemistry, crystal field theory is useful in understanding and predicting the behavior of transition metal complexes.

<!-- SEG:m68844:title:fs-idm148283216-title -->
Crystal Field Theory

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm133288048 -->
To explain the observed behavior of transition metal complexes (such as how colors arise), a model involving electrostatic interactions between the electrons from the ligands and the electrons in the unhybridized [[i:d]] orbitals of the central metal atom has been developed. This electrostatic model is {{term}}crystal field theory{{/term}} (CFT). It allows us to understand, interpret, and predict the colors, magnetic behavior, and some structures of coordination compounds of transition metals.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm89530000 -->
CFT focuses on the nonbonding electrons on the central metal ion in coordination complexes not on the metal-ligand bonds. Like valence bond theory, CFT tells only part of the story of the behavior of complexes. However, it tells the part that valence bond theory does not. In its pure form, CFT ignores any covalent bonding between ligands and metal ions. Both the ligand and the metal are treated as infinitesimally small point charges.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm52652048 -->
All electrons are negative, so the electrons donated from the ligands will repel the electrons of the central metal. Let us consider the behavior of the electrons in the unhybridized [[i:d]] orbitals in an octahedral complex. The five [[i:d]] orbitals consist of lobe-shaped regions and are arranged in space, as shown in [[xref:CNX_Chem_19_03_Dorbital]]. In an octahedral complex, the six ligands coordinate along the axes.

<!-- SEG:m68844:caption:CNX_Chem_19_03_Dorbital-caption -->
The directional characteristics of the five [[i:d]] orbitals are shown here. The shaded portions indicate the phase of the orbitals. The ligands (L) coordinate along the axes. For clarity, the ligands have been omitted from the [[MATH:1]] orbital so that the axis labels could be shown.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm103130128 -->
In an uncomplexed metal ion in the gas phase, the electrons are distributed among the five [[i:d]] orbitals in accord with Hund's rule because the orbitals all have the same energy. However, when ligands coordinate to a metal ion, the energies of the [[i:d]] orbitals are no longer the same.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm39708976 -->
In octahedral complexes, the lobes in two of the five [[i:d]] orbitals, the [[MATH:2]] and [[MATH:3]] orbitals, point toward the ligands ([[xref:CNX_Chem_19_03_Dorbital]]). These two orbitals are called the {{term}}[[i:e]][[sub:[[i:g]]]] orbitals{{/term}} (the symbol actually refers to the symmetry of the orbitals, but we will use it as a convenient name for these two orbitals in an octahedral complex). The other three orbitals, the [[i:d[[sub:xy]]]], [[i:d[[sub:xz]]]], and [[i:d[[sub:yz]]]] orbitals, have lobes that point between the ligands and are called the {{term}}[[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] orbitals{{/term}} (again, the symbol really refers to the symmetry of the orbitals). As six ligands approach the metal ion along the axes of the octahedron, their point charges repel the electrons in the [[i:d]] orbitals of the metal ion. However, the repulsions between the electrons in the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals (the [[MATH:4]] and [[MATH:5]] orbitals) and the ligands are greater than the repulsions between the electrons in the [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] orbitals (the [[i:d[[sub:zy]]]], [[i:d[[sub:xz]]]], and [[i:d[[sub:yz]]]] orbitals) and the ligands. This is because the lobes of the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals point directly at the ligands, whereas the lobes of the [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] orbitals point between them. Thus, electrons in the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals of the metal ion in an octahedral complex have higher potential energies than those of electrons in the [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] orbitals. The difference in energy may be represented as shown in [[xref:CNX_Chem_19_03_CFSE]].

<!-- SEG:m68844:caption:CNX_Chem_19_03_CFSE-caption -->
In octahedral complexes, the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals are destabilized (higher in energy) compared to the [[i:t]][[sub:2g]] orbitals because the ligands interact more strongly with the [[i:d]] orbitals at which they are pointed directly.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm71803888 -->
The difference in energy between the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] and the [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] orbitals is called the {{term}}crystal field splitting{{/term}} and is symbolized by {{term}}Δ[[sub:oct]]{{/term}}, where oct stands for octahedral.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm102136864 -->
The magnitude of Δ[[sub:oct]] depends on many factors, including the nature of the six ligands located around the central metal ion, the charge on the metal, and whether the metal is using 3[[i:d]], 4[[i:d]], or 5[[i:d]] orbitals. Different ligands produce different crystal field splittings. The increasing crystal field splitting produced by ligands is expressed in the {{term}}spectrochemical series{{/term}}, a short version of which is given here:

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm118198736 -->
In this series, ligands on the left cause small crystal field splittings and are {{term}}weak-field ligands{{/term}}, whereas those on the right cause larger splittings and are {{term}}strong-field ligands{{/term}}. Thus, the Δ[[sub:oct]] value for an octahedral complex with iodide ligands (I[[sup:−]]) is much smaller than the Δ[[sub:oct]] value for the same metal with cyanide ligands (CN[[sup:−]]).

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm52923792 -->
Electrons in the [[i:d]] orbitals follow the aufbau (“filling up”) principle, which says that the orbitals will be filled to give the lowest total energy, just as in main group chemistry. When two electrons occupy the same orbital, the like charges repel each other. The energy needed to pair up two electrons in a single orbital is called the {{term}}pairing energy (P){{/term}}. Electrons will always singly occupy each orbital in a degenerate set before pairing. P is similar in magnitude to Δ[[sub:oct]]. When electrons fill the [[i:d]] orbitals, the relative magnitudes of Δ[[sub:oct]] and P determine which orbitals will be occupied.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm90828912 -->
In [Fe(CN)[[sub:6]]][[sup:4−]], the strong field of six cyanide ligands produces a large Δ[[sub:oct]]. Under these conditions, the electrons require less energy to pair than they require to be excited to the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals (Δ[[sub:oct]] > P). The six 3[[i:d]] electrons of the Fe[[sup:2+]] ion pair in the three [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] orbitals ([[xref:CNX_Chem_19_03_Lowhigh]]). Complexes in which the electrons are paired because of the large crystal field splitting are called {{term}}low-spin complexes{{/term}} because the number of unpaired electrons (spins) is minimized.

<!-- SEG:m68844:caption:CNX_Chem_19_03_Lowhigh-caption -->
Iron(II) complexes have six electrons in the 5[[i:d]] orbitals. In the absence of a crystal field, the orbitals are degenerate. For coordination complexes with strong-field ligands such as [Fe(CN)[[sub:6]]][[sup:4−]], Δ[[sub:oct]] is greater than P, and the electrons pair in the lower energy [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] orbitals before occupying the [[i:eg]] orbitals. With weak-field ligands such as H[[sub:2]]O, the ligand field splitting is less than the pairing energy, Δ[[sub:oct]] less than P, so the electrons occupy all [[i:d]] orbitals singly before any pairing occurs.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm103034960 -->
In [Fe(H[[sub:2]]O)[[sub:6]]][[sup:2+]], on the other hand, the weak field of the water molecules produces only a small crystal field splitting (Δ[[sub:oct]] &lt; P). Because it requires less energy for the electrons to occupy the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals than to pair together, there will be an electron in each of the five 3[[i:d]] orbitals before pairing occurs. For the six [[i:d]] electrons on the iron(II) center in [Fe(H[[sub:2]]O)[[sub:6]]][[sup:2+]], there will be one pair of electrons and four unpaired electrons ([[xref:CNX_Chem_19_03_Lowhigh]]). Complexes such as the [Fe(H[[sub:2]]O)[[sub:6]]][[sup:2+]] ion, in which the electrons are unpaired because the crystal field splitting is not large enough to cause them to pair, are called {{term}}high-spin complexes{{/term}} because the number of unpaired electrons (spins) is maximized.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm124162784 -->
A similar line of reasoning shows why the [Fe(CN)[[sub:6]]][[sup:3−]] ion is a low-spin complex with only one unpaired electron, whereas both the [Fe(H[[sub:2]]O)[[sub:6]]][[sup:3+]] and [FeF[[sub:6]]][[sup:3−]] ions are high-spin complexes with five unpaired electrons.

CFT for Other Geometries

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm40722368 -->
CFT is applicable to molecules in geometries other than octahedral. In octahedral complexes, remember that the lobes of the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] set point directly at the ligands. For tetrahedral complexes, the [[i:d]] orbitals remain in place, but now we have only four ligands located between the axes ([[xref:CNX_Chem_19_03_TetCube]]). None of the orbitals points directly at the tetrahedral ligands. However, the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] set (along the Cartesian axes) overlaps with the ligands less than does the [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] set. By analogy with the octahedral case, predict the energy diagram for the [[i:d]] orbitals in a tetrahedral crystal field. To avoid confusion, the octahedral [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] set becomes a tetrahedral [[i:e]] set, and the octahedral [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] set becomes a [[i:t]][[sub:2]] set.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para-title:fs-idp69600720-title -->
Solution

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idp69600720 -->
Since CFT is based on electrostatic repulsion, the orbitals closer to the ligands will be destabilized and raised in energy relative to the other set of orbitals. The splitting is less than for octahedral complexes because the overlap is less, so Δ[[sub:tet]] is usually small [[MATH:6]]

Magnetic Moments of Molecules and Ions

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idp59322128 -->
Experimental evidence of magnetic measurements supports the theory of high- and low-spin complexes. Remember that molecules such as O[[sub:2]] that contain unpaired electrons are paramagnetic. Paramagnetic substances are attracted to magnetic fields. Many transition metal complexes have unpaired electrons and hence are paramagnetic. Molecules such as N[[sub:2]] and ions such as Na[[sup:+]] and [Fe(CN)[[sub:6]]][[sup:4−]] that contain no unpaired electrons are diamagnetic. Diamagnetic substances have a slight tendency to be repelled by magnetic fields.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm122157040 -->
When an electron in an atom or ion is unpaired, the magnetic moment due to its spin makes the entire atom or ion paramagnetic. The size of the magnetic moment of a system containing unpaired electrons is related directly to the number of such electrons: the greater the number of unpaired electrons, the larger the magnetic moment. Therefore, the observed magnetic moment is used to determine the number of unpaired electrons present. The measured magnetic moment of low-spin [[i:d]][[sup:6]] [Fe(CN)[[sub:6]]][[sup:4−]] confirms that iron is diamagnetic, whereas high-spin [[i:d]][[sup:6]] [Fe(H[[sub:2]]O)[[sub:6]]][[sup:2+]] has four unpaired electrons with a magnetic moment that confirms this arrangement.

<!-- SEG:m68844:title:fs-idm217884976-title -->
Colors of Transition Metal Complexes

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm81753104 -->
When atoms or molecules absorb light at the proper frequency, their electrons are excited to higher-energy orbitals. For many main group atoms and molecules, the absorbed photons are in the ultraviolet range of the electromagnetic spectrum, which cannot be detected by the human eye. For coordination compounds, the energy difference between the [[i:d]] orbitals often allows photons in the visible range to be absorbed.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm22856880 -->
The human eye perceives a mixture of all the colors, in the proportions present in sunlight, as white light. Complementary colors, those located across from each other on a color wheel, are also used in color vision. The eye perceives a mixture of two complementary colors, in the proper proportions, as white light. Likewise, when a color is missing from white light, the eye sees its complement. For example, when red photons are absorbed from white light, the eyes see the color green. When violet photons are removed from white light, the eyes see lemon yellow. The blue color of the [Cu(NH[[sub:3]])[[sub:4]]][[sup:2+]] ion results because this ion absorbs orange and red light, leaving the complementary colors of blue and green ([[xref:CNX_Chem_19_03_Lightper]]).

<!-- SEG:m68844:caption:CNX_Chem_19_03_Lightper-caption -->
(a) An object is black if it absorbs all colors of light. If it reflects all colors of light, it is white. An object has a color if it absorbs all colors except one, such as this yellow strip. The strip also appears yellow if it absorbs the complementary color from white light (in this case, indigo). (b) Complementary colors are located directly across from one another on the color wheel. (c) A solution of [Cu(NH[[sub:3]])[[sub:4]]][[sup:2+]] ions absorbs red and orange light, so the transmitted light appears as the complementary color, blue.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm73744320 -->
In general, strong-field ligands cause a large split in the energies of [[i:d]] orbitals of the central metal atom (large Δ[[sub:oct]]). Transition metal coordination compounds with these ligands are yellow, orange, or red because they absorb higher-energy violet or blue light. On the other hand, coordination compounds of transition metals with weak-field ligands are often blue-green, blue, or indigo because they absorb lower-energy yellow, orange, or red light.

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm243292976 -->
A coordination compound of the Cu[[sup:+]] ion has a [[i:d]][[sup:10]] configuration, and all the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals are filled. To excite an electron to a higher level, such as the 4[[i:p]] orbital, photons of very high energy are necessary. This energy corresponds to very short wavelengths in the ultraviolet region of the spectrum. No visible light is absorbed, so the eye sees no change, and the compound appears white or colorless. A solution containing [Cu(CN)[[sub:2]]][[sup:−]], for example, is colorless. On the other hand, octahedral Cu[[sup:2+]] complexes have a vacancy in the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals, and electrons can be excited to this level. The wavelength (energy) of the light absorbed corresponds to the visible part of the spectrum, and Cu[[sup:2+]] complexes are almost always colored—blue, blue-green violet, or yellow ([[xref:CNX_Chem_19_03_Cu]]). Although CFT successfully describes many properties of coordination complexes, molecular orbital explanations (beyond the introductory scope provided here) are required to understand fully the behavior of coordination complexes.

<!-- SEG:m68844:title:fs-idm106302704-title -->
Key Concepts and Summary

<!-- SEG:m68844:para:fs-idm138698112 -->
Crystal field theory treats interactions between the electrons on the metal and the ligands as a simple electrostatic effect. The presence of the ligands near the metal ion changes the energies of the metal [[i:d]] orbitals relative to their energies in the free ion. Both the color and the magnetic properties of a complex can be attributed to this crystal field splitting. The magnitude of the splitting (Δ[[sub:oct]]) depends on the nature of the ligands bonded to the metal. Strong-field ligands produce large splitting and favor low-spin complexes, in which the [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] orbitals are completely filled before any electrons occupy the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals. Weak-field ligands favor formation of high-spin complexes. The [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] and the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals are singly occupied before any are doubly occupied.

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idm99275552-term -->
crystal field splitting (Δoct)

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idm99275552-def -->
difference in energy between the [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] and [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] sets or [[i:t]] and [[i:e]] sets of orbitals

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idm31354480-term -->
crystal field theory

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idm31354480-def -->
model that explains the energies of the orbitals in transition metals in terms of electrostatic interactions with the ligands but does not include metal ligand bonding

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idm148194896-term -->
eg orbitals

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idm148194896-def -->
set of two [[i:d]] orbitals that are oriented on the Cartesian axes for coordination complexes; in octahedral complexes, they are higher in energy than the [[i:t]][[sub:2[[i:g]]]] orbitals

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idm132411312-term -->
geometric isomers

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idm132411312-def -->
isomers that differ in the way in which atoms are oriented in space relative to each other, leading to different physical and chemical properties

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idm33844976-term -->
high-spin complex

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idm33844976-def -->
complex in which the electrons maximize the total electron spin by singly populating all of the orbitals before pairing two electrons into the lower-energy orbitals

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idp121047712-term -->
low-spin complex

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idp121047712-def -->
complex in which the electrons minimize the total electron spin by pairing in the lower-energy orbitals before populating the higher-energy orbitals

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idm65480336-term -->
pairing energy (P)

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idm65480336-def -->
energy required to place two electrons with opposite spins into a single orbital

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idm145242928-term -->
spectrochemical series

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idm145242928-def -->
ranking of ligands according to the magnitude of the crystal field splitting they induce

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idm24403040-term -->
strong-field ligand

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idm24403040-def -->
ligand that causes larger crystal field splittings

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idm104611088-term -->
t2g orbitals

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idm104611088-def -->
set of three [[i:d]] orbitals aligned between the Cartesian axes for coordination complexes; in octahedral complexes, they are lowered in energy compared to the [[i:e[[sub:g]]]] orbitals according to CFT

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-term:fs-idp10927328-term -->
weak-field ligand

<!-- SEG:m68844:glossary-def:fs-idp10927328-def -->
ligand that causes small crystal field splittings
